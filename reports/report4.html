Algoma University Winter 2025 Co-op Work-Term Report

Abstract/Introduction

Welcome to my work-term report, detailing my experience as a Research Assistant at Algoma University from January to April 2025. This report offers a comprehensive overview of my contributions, responsibilities, and achievements during this period.

Within these pages, you'll find insights about Algoma University as an employer, accompanied by relevant visuals that enhance the narrative. Additionally, the report delves into my job description and learning objectives, providing a holistic understanding of my time at the university.

Throughout this work term, I had the unique opportunity to collaborate with two professors on distinct research projects: Professor Randy Lin at Algoma University's Brampton Campus and Professor Yan Yan at the University of Guelph's School of Computer Science. This dual experience allowed me to engage with cutting-edge research in 3D mesh processing, neural radiance fields (NeRF), and bioinformatics, significantly broadening my technical expertise and research capabilities.

Information about the Employer

Algoma University, established in 1965 as Algoma College and achieving university status in 2008, holds a distinctive position in Canada's academic landscape. Located in Sault Ste. Marie, Ontario, with additional campuses in Brampton and Timmins, this institution combines a rich historical legacy with a forward-thinking approach to education and research.

The university's origins are deeply intertwined with the former Shingwauk Residential School site, leading to a unique mandate among Canadian universities: a special mission to cultivate cross-cultural learning between Indigenous and non-Indigenous communities. This commitment is reflected in Algoma's Center for Social Justice and Good Governance and its focus on Anishinaabe education, history, and culture.

With a student body of approximately 3,000 students across its three campuses, Algoma University offers an intimate learning environment with a student-to-faculty ratio that facilitates personalized education and mentorship. The university's academic structure encompasses three faculties: Science and Technology, Social Sciences, and Humanities, providing diverse educational pathways to students from across Canada and around the world.

The School of Computer Science at Algoma University, where I conducted my research, is renowned for its innovative approaches to computational problems and its emphasis on applied research. The department fosters an environment where students and faculty collaborate on cutting-edge research projects, ranging from artificial intelligence and machine learning to computer graphics and bioinformatics.

Algoma's Brampton campus, where I was based during my work term, represents the university's expansion into the Greater Toronto Area. Established in 2010 in partnership with the City of Brampton, this campus has grown significantly, particularly in technology-focused programs. The strategic location within the tech corridor of Southern Ontario provides unique opportunities for collaboration with industry partners and research institutions.

In recent years, Algoma University has made substantial investments in research infrastructure, particularly in computational sciences. These investments have enabled faculty members like Professor Randy Lin to pursue innovative research in areas such as 3D mesh processing, neural networks, and computational modeling, creating valuable opportunities for students to engage in meaningful research experiences.

During my work term at Algoma University, I was primarily assigned to Professor Randy Lin's research group at the Brampton campus, focusing on 3D mesh processing and machine learning applications. This research involved developing methods for decimating 3D meshes, training machine learning models on both decimated and non-decimated meshes, and reconstructing high-quality 3D models using Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF).

Simultaneously, I collaborated with Professor Yan Yan at the University of Guelph on bioinformatics research, initially working on a large language model called "AgroNT" for gene expression level prediction and later pivoting to developing a Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS) toolkit. This unique dual-research position provided me with diverse experiences across multiple computational domains, enriching my technical skills and research capabilities.

Reflection 1

While my original goal involved developing a CLI pipeline for AgroNT, circumstances necessitated a pivot to another machine learning project under Professor Randy Lin's supervision. Instead of the intended gene expression prediction model, I successfully designed and implemented a CLI pipeline for a 3D mesh downscaler and upscaler project.

Using Python, I developed a system that automatically downscales/distorts 3D meshes from open-license databases like ShapeNet to create supervised training datasets for machine learning models such as NeRFs. The pipeline incorporated various open-source tools including pymeshlab and blender, demonstrating my ability to adapt to changing project requirements while maintaining technical rigor.

Although my involvement concluded at the NeRF dataset adaptation and conversion stage due to budgetary constraints, the project achieved significant progress. Professor Lin expressed high satisfaction with the completed work, validating that I successfully met the core technical objectives of designing and implementing a functional CLI pipeline for a machine learning application, albeit in a different domain than originally planned.

This experience highlighted the importance of flexibility in technical development while maintaining focus on delivering a reliable, well-documented system that meets user needs. The skills developed during this process—particularly in pipeline design, system integration, and technical documentation—will prove valuable in future software engineering endeavors regardless of the specific application domain.

Job Description

As a Research Assistant at Algoma University's Brampton Campus, my role encompassed a diverse range of responsibilities that spanned both technical development and academic research. Working primarily with Professor Randy Lin on 3D mesh processing and machine learning projects, while also collaborating with Professor Yan Yan on bioinformatics research, my position demanded versatility, technical depth, and strong communication skills.

  • 3D Mesh Pipeline Development: Designed and implemented a comprehensive CLI pipeline for downscaling and upscaling 3D meshes, creating a system that automated the generation of training datasets for Neural Radiance Field (NeRF) models.
  • Machine Learning Integration: Integrated various machine learning components with the 3D mesh pipeline, including experimentation with NeRF architectures for high-quality 3D reconstruction from decimated models.
  • Bioinformatics Research: Contributed to the development of the AgroNT large language model for gene expression prediction, and later pivoted to developing components for a GWAS (Genome-Wide Association Studies) toolkit.
  • Data Collection and Management: Gathered, organized, and maintained databases of 2D images and 3D models from sources like ShapeNet, ensuring proper data preparation for machine learning applications.
  • Research Implementation: Tested and examined existing workflows for 3D model generation from 2D images, providing critical analysis and identifying opportunities for improvement.
  • Documentation: Generated comprehensive documentation for all projects, including technical specifications, user guides, and research notes.
  • Research Manuscript Preparation: Contributed to academic writing summarizing project work, with preparations for conference paper submission.
  • Progress Reporting: Prepared regular detailed reports on project progress, methodologies, and results for both supervising professors.
  • Software Testing and Quality Assurance: Developed and implemented testing methodologies to ensure software reliability and performance across different platforms and use cases.
  • Literature Review: Conducted extensive reviews of academic literature related to NeRF technologies, 3D reconstruction, and bioinformatics applications.

The most captivating aspect of this role was the interdisciplinary nature of the research, bridging computer graphics, machine learning, and bioinformatics. Working at the intersection of these fields provided a unique perspective on how computational techniques can be applied across diverse domains.

This position required a solid foundation in programming languages including Python, Golang, and R, as well as familiarity with machine learning frameworks like PyTorch. My academic background in Software Engineering at the University of Guelph provided an excellent theoretical foundation, particularly in software design principles and testing methodologies. However, many specialized skills—such as 3D mesh manipulation with pymeshlab and advanced PyTorch implementations for NeRF models—were developed on the job through self-directed learning and mentorship from both professors.

The dual-research nature of this position added complexity but also enriched the experience, allowing me to apply similar computational principles to very different problem domains. This versatility will undoubtedly be valuable as I progress in my software engineering career, where adaptability and cross-domain knowledge transfer are increasingly important.

Conclusions

As I reflect on my work term at Algoma University's Brampton Campus, several key insights emerge that have profoundly shaped my development as a software engineer and researcher. The experience of working simultaneously on two distinct research projects—3D mesh processing with Professor Lin and bioinformatics applications with Professor Yan—provided an invaluable perspective on the versatility of computational methods across different domains.

The 3D mesh downscaling and upscaling project demonstrated the transformative potential of neural networks in computer graphics, particularly for reconstructing high-quality 3D models from limited data. Working with cutting-edge technologies like Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) revealed both the remarkable capabilities and current limitations of these approaches, highlighting the dynamic nature of this research field. The process of building a comprehensive pipeline for this project reinforced the importance of robust software engineering practices, even in research contexts.

Similarly, my contributions to the GWAS toolkit underscored the critical role that computational tools play in advancing biological research. This project illuminated how well-designed software can significantly accelerate scientific discovery by making sophisticated analytical methods accessible to researchers with varying levels of computational expertise. It also reinforced my appreciation for the interdisciplinary collaboration that drives innovation at the intersection of computer science and other fields.

Perhaps the most significant takeaway from this work term was the development of adaptability as a core professional skill. When faced with pivoting research directions or encountering unexpected technical challenges, the ability to quickly acquire new knowledge and apply it effectively proved essential. This adaptability, combined with deepened technical expertise in areas like Python development, 3D modeling, and machine learning implementation, has substantially enriched my professional capabilities.

As I move forward in my software engineering career, I carry with me not only enhanced technical skills but also a deeper understanding of the research process and its inherent unpredictability. The experience of contributing to projects with potential real-world applications—whether in computer graphics or genomics—has reinforced my passion for developing technology that addresses meaningful problems and advances human knowledge.
