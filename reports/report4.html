Algoma University Winter 2025 Co-op Work-Term Report

Abstract/Introduction

Welcome to my co-op work-term report for work-term 4. I will be describing information about my employer, my experience working for them, my job responsibilities, listing my learning goals and my reflections of them, and acknowledging the people that made this experience possible.

I worked as an undergraduate research assistant in the computer science department at Algoma. My contributions involved 3 separate research projects in computing: 2 of them being cutting-edge machine learning projects, and the remaining being a genome-wide association study (GWAS) project.

Information about the Employer

Algoma University is not a common Canadian university because of its deep ties to the "Indigenous peoples" of Canada. By "Indigenous peoples", I am referring to the 3 legally recognized groups of people: First Nations, Inuit, and Metis, in Canada. Notably, the university's Sault Ste. Marie campus is built on the former Shingwauk Indian Residential School site as a project to foster the growth and development of the Shingwauk site. This Shingwauk site used to be home to one of Canada's many Indian Residential Schools, which makes the history of Algoma's Salt Ste. Marie campus even more culturally and historically significant.

Algoma University has 3 campuses: Sault Ste. Marie, Timmins, and Brampton. Sault Ste. Marie and Timmins are the university's "Northen Ontario" locations whereas Brampton is the university's urban campus. The university's main campus is the Sault Ste. Marie one, which is why it is referred to as a "Northen Ontario" university. Technically, Professor Randy Lin works at the Bramptom campus, but I never managed to work on campus because I was working remotely for my work-term. That being said, the Brampton campus is situated in downtown Brampton, making it within reach of Toronto, and well within the confines of the Greater Toronto Area (GTA).

Professor Randy Lin has a very diverse academic computing background: bioinformatics and computer graphics mainly come to mind, if you read his published research. I worked with him on a computer graphics project that uses machine learning techniques such as Neural Radiance Field (NeRF) models to approximate recreations of existing and new 3D meshes. Computer graphics is a developing subfield of computer science, and getting to integrate novel machine learning approaches to it was the highlight of my work-term. His expertise, alongside the expertise of his graduate student, Bamikole "Banki" Adewale, were invaluable in helping me learn so much more about the ins and outs of computer graphics, and the power machine learning has to enhance this field, especially if used responsibly and ingeniously. From what I remember, the project would eventually be used to readily and cheaply construct realistic 3D environments in virtual reality settings by Algoma's psychology department. Thusly, this research project was an interdisciplinary research project, especially in the application side of it.

While working for Professor Lin and Banki on the computer graphics project, I was focused on finding useful bioinformatics applications for a large language model (LLM) trained on bioinformatics datasets, called "AgroNT" under Professor Yan Yan's co-supervision. Professor Yan is one of the experts in bioinformatics at the School of Computer Science (SoCS) at the University of Guelph (UoG), and has co-authored multiple papers with Professor Lin in the past, which is how the acquaintance was made.

The pipeline incorporated various open-source tools including pymeshlab and blender, demonstrating my ability to adapt to changing project requirements while maintaining technical rigor.

Although my involvement concluded at the NeRF dataset adaptation and conversion stage due to budgetary constraints, the project achieved significant progress. Professor Lin expressed high satisfaction with the completed work, validating that I successfully met the core technical objectives of designing and implementing a functional CLI pipeline for a machine learning application, albeit in a different domain than originally planned.

This experience highlighted the importance of flexibility in technical development while maintaining focus on delivering a reliable, well-documented system that meets user needs. Job Description

My work under Professor Lin and Yan's co-supervision involved a sizeable amount of technical (hard) and non-technical (soft) skills. The work was highly intriguing, and often challenging and novel, thus making my work-term more valuable as a learning experience. While it was regrettable the AgroNT project was killed towards the end of my work-term, I learned so much about deep learning and core bioinformatics techniques through it, and it paved the way for a more pragmatic and promising genome-wide association studies (GWAS) project right after its untimely end. In any case, below is a list of my job responsibilities that should serve as a useful summary of what I did for work during the work-term.

  • Gather, create, and maintain 2D image databases for the 3D model.
  • Test and examine the existing workflows for generating 3D models using 2D images.
  • Gather data and test existing bioinformatics models for analysis.
  • Create and maintain the research group website for the projects.
  • Generate documentations during the projects.
  • And, write research manuscript summarizing the project work.

The key takeaway from working with various machine learning models in this work-term was: machine learning is mainly about compressing tremendous amounts of data into more digestable forms, and developing approximation functions to model many of life's phenomena. For example: NeRFs are a way to model reconstructing complex 3D scenes as closely as possible, and it uses a methematical model to approximate the task of recreating complex 3D scenes from training datasets. It is like asking a machine to look at a picture depicting a 3D scene, memorize the contents of the picture, and recreate it from memory without looking or referring to the original learning photograph. The mathematical model used here allows the computer (i.e., the "machine") to learn patterns from the input dataset so it can recreate the scene from memory as faithfully as possible.

Of course, and this being an academic position, I had to complete a lot of reading, especially of dense technical scientific literature in computer science and biology. I will say: the more experience I get in academia, the more I realize how poorly written much of contemporary academic literature really is. I lost count over the amount of times I got needlessly confused by verbose wording and phrasing, and the strange use of overly sophisticated terminology when simpler alternatives would work. Writing is really an art, and the best academics prove that it is regularly. You can know just about everything in a field, but being able to clearly communicate it to others in the written form is another challenge entirely. I hope if I continue in academia that I continue to develop this integral skill to pave the way for more accessible and useful science, and ultimately make the computing academic field better as a result.

All in all, the work-term was an excellent learning experience, and I would do it all over again, if I could.

Conclusions

I worked as a researcher in computing at Algoma University from early January 2025 to late April 2025 for my fourth work-term. This job was a full-time role where I worked on deep learning models and GWAS toolkits for bioinformatics, and on a machine learning system for downscaling and upscaling real 3D meshes at will for a virtual reality project. My work incorporated learnings in bioinformatics, computer graphics, and machine learning, which are all fascinating subsets of the greater computer science field.

I enjoyed this work-term as a learning and professional experience because of the novel subfields of computing it made me work in. There was ample challenge and learning to be completed, especially on the job, and it paid off in my current and future knowledge, skills, and aptitude (KSA) as a software developer and computer science researcher.

Based on what I have learned thus far from this work-term, I cannot underestimate the value of clear communication, especially in technical fields where poor writing is more often the norm rather than the exception. If there were only one thing I could take away from this work-term, it would be this lesson.
